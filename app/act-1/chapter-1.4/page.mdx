# Chapter 1.4: Overflow Logic

<ChapterCard>
### The Briefing: The Boundaries of Hardware
In an $n$-bit 2's complement system, the valid range of representable numbers is strictly $[-2^{n-1}, +2^{n-1} - 1]$. 
When two numbers of the *same sign* are added, the result may fall outside this range. This condition is called **Overflow**. 
Hardware detects overflow using a simple Boolean function on the carry bits of the Most Significant Bit (MSB):
$V = C\_{in} \oplus C\_{out}$

Where:
* $C\_{in}$ is the carry entering the sign bit (MSB).
* $C\_{out}$ is the carry leaving the sign bit.

If $V = 1$, overflow has occurred, and the sign bit of the result is mathematically invalid.
</ChapterCard>

<GateTrap>
**Trap: Overflow with Opposite Signs**
A common trick in GATE problems is asking if overflow can occur when adding a positive and a negative number. **It is physically impossible.** Overflow ONLY occurs when adding two positive numbers (resulting in a negative sign bit) or adding two negative numbers (resulting in a positive sign bit). Do not waste time calculating carry bits if the operands have different signs.
</GateTrap>

<ArchitectShortcut>
**Shortcut: The Sign-Bit Inspector**
You do not need to calculate $C\_{in}$ and $C\_{out}$ to determine overflow if you can see the result.
Just look at the sign bits!
If $A\_{sign} = B\_{sign}$ and $Result\_{sign} \neq A\_{sign}$, then Overflow = 1.
Example: Adding two positives (0 + 0) and getting a negative (1) implies the magnitude has bled into the sign bit. 
</ArchitectShortcut>
