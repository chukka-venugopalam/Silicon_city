# Chapter 1.2: Fixed Point Architecture

<ChapterCard>
### The Briefing: Signed Numbers and Complements
The ALU does not intrinsically understand negative numbers. To perform subtraction, the hardware reuses the addition circuitry by transforming the subtrahend into its complement form.
For an $n$-bit system:
* **Sign-Magnitude:** MSB is the sign (0 = pos, 1 = neg). Two representations of zero (+0, -0).
* **1's Complement:** Invert all bits of the positive number. Still suffers from $\pm 0$. Range: $-(2^{n-1} - 1)$ to $+(2^{n-1} - 1)$.
* **2's Complement:** The industry standard. Calculated by adding 1 to the 1's complement. Single unique zero. Asymmetric range: $-(2^{n-1})$ to $+(2^{n-1} - 1)$.
</ChapterCard>

<GateTrap>
**Trap: Sign Extension Failure**
When converting a smaller bit-width signed number to a larger one (e.g., 4-bit to 8-bit), students often pad with leading zeros. This destroys the value of negative numbers in 2's complement. You must **Sign Extend**: replicate the Most Significant Bit (MSB).
Example: 4-bit $-3$ is `1101`. As an 8-bit number, it is `11111101`, NOT `00001101` (which is $+13$).
</GateTrap>

<ArchitectShortcut>
**Shortcut: The Instant 2's Complement**
To find the 2's complement of a binary number without inverting and adding 1:
Scan the number from **Right to Left** (LSB to MSB). Keep all zeros and the first '1' exactly as they are. Invert all subsequent bits to the left of that first '1'.
*Example:* `1010100` $\rightarrow$ Right-to-left: keep `100`, invert the rest $\rightarrow$ `0101100`.
</ArchitectShortcut>
