# Chapter 1.21: Combinational Logic - Adders

<ChapterCard>
### The Briefing: Combinational Arithmetic
Combinational logic circuits are time-independent. Their outputs are pure Boolean functions of their current inputs, with no memory of past states.

The fundamental building blocks of ALUs are Adders:
*   **Half Adder:** Adds two 1-bit numbers ($A, B$). Produces Sum ($S = A \oplus B$) and Carry ($C = AB$). It cannot accept a carry from a previous stage.
*   **Full Adder:** Adds three 1-bit numbers ($A, B, C\_{in}$). Produces Sum ($S = A \oplus B \oplus C\_{in}$) and Carry Out ($C\_{out} = AB + C\_{in}(A \oplus B)$).

A Full Adder can be constructed using two Half Adders and one OR gate.
</ChapterCard>

<GateTrap>
**Trap: Delay Cascades**
In a Ripple Carry Adder (RCA) constructed from $N$ Full Adders, the Sum for bit $N$ cannot be validated until the Carry from bit $N-1$ has propagated through. The critical path is entirely dictated by the Carry logic, not the Sum logic. If an exam asks for the total propagation delay of a 4-bit adder, you must calculate: $Delay = (N-1) \times t\_{carry} + \max(t\_{carry}, t\_{sum})$. Do not just multiply $N$ by the total gate delay of a single Full Adder.
</GateTrap>

<ArchitectShortcut>
**Shortcut: The Subtract-by-Add Hack**
To implement $A - B$ using an existing adder circuit, you do not need a dedicated subtractor. Use 2's Complement: $A - B = A + (-B) = A + (\text{NOT } B) + 1$.
Hardware implementation: Invert all bits of $B$ using XOR gates (with a control signal of 1) and feed that same control signal `1` into the $C\_{in}$ of the first Full Adder. You now have an Adder/Subtractor with zero extra delay on the critical path.
</ArchitectShortcut>
