# Chapter 1.1: Numerical Origins

<ChapterCard>
### The Briefing: Radix Systems and Base Conversions
The foundation of digital logic rests entirely on radix (base) systems. A number system with base $r$ uses $r$ distinct symbols. The value of any digit depends on its position, governed by the polynomial expansion:
$N = d_n r^n + d\_{n-1} r^{n-1} + ... + d_0 r^0 + d\_{-1} r^{-1} + ...$

In computer architecture, we primarily operate in:
* **Binary (Base-2):** The physical logic state (0V or 5V).
* **Hexadecimal (Base-16):** The human-readable compression of binary streams.

Conversion between these bases is not just mathematical; it is the fundamental translation between human instruction and silicon execution.
</ChapterCard>

<GateTrap>
**Trap: Fractional Base Conversion Loss**
When converting fractional decimal numbers to binary by repeated multiplication by 2, students often calculate endlessly. In GATE exams, floating-point precision questions usually limit to 3 or 4 bits. **Never assume a fraction will terminate in binary** (e.g., $0.1\_{10}$ is a non-terminating binary fraction). Know when to truncate based on the options provided.
</GateTrap>

<ArchitectShortcut>
**Shortcut: The 8-4-2-1 Matrix**
Do not use repeated division for Base-10 to Base-2 conversions if the number is less than 256. Simply subtract the largest power of 2.
For Hex to Binary, never convert to Decimal first. Each Hex digit corresponds exactly to a 4-bit binary nibble.
$A\_{16} \rightarrow 1010\_{2}$, $F\_{16} \rightarrow 1111\_{2}$. 
Direct substitution saves critical seconds.
</ArchitectShortcut>
