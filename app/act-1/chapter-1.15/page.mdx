# Chapter 1.15: Inference Rules

<ChapterCard>
### The Briefing: The Architecture of Proof
Inference rules are the atomic steps used to build a valid mathematical proof. An argument is valid if the conjunction of its premises implies its conclusion (i.e., $(P_1 \land P_2 \land ... \land P_n) \rightarrow C$ is a tautology).
Key inference rules include:
* **Modus Ponens:** $P \rightarrow Q$, $P$ $\vdash Q$
* **Modus Tollens:** $P \rightarrow Q$, $\neg Q$ $\vdash \neg P$
* **Hypothetical Syllogism:** $P \rightarrow Q$, $Q \rightarrow R$ $\vdash P \rightarrow R$
* **Disjunctive Syllogism:** $P \lor Q$, $\neg P$ $\vdash Q$

A proof is simply a sequence of these verified structural transformations.
</ChapterCard>

<GateTrap>
**Trap: The Converse and Inverse Fallacies**
GATE will intentionally construct arguments using standard human cognitive biases. 
1. **Fallacy of Affirming the Consequent:** $P \rightarrow Q$, $Q$ $\vdash P$. (INVALID)
2. **Fallacy of Denying the Antecedent:** $P \rightarrow Q$, $\neg P$ $\vdash \neg Q$. (INVALID)
Just because it rains when it's cloudy ($P \rightarrow Q$), knowing it is raining ($Q$) does not prove it is cloudy ($P$). Do not let intuition override structural logic.
</GateTrap>

<ArchitectShortcut>
**Shortcut: Transitivity Collapsing**
In a question with multiple chained implications (e.g., $A \rightarrow B, B \rightarrow C, C \rightarrow D$), you can immediately collapse them into $A \rightarrow D$. If you are given $A$ as a premise, you can instantly conclude $D$. If you are given $\neg D$ as a premise, you can instantly conclude $\neg A$ via Modus Tollens. Ignore all intermediate variables.
</ArchitectShortcut>
