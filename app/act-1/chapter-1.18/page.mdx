# Chapter 1.18: Natural Language to Formal Logic

<ChapterCard>
### The Briefing: The Compiler of the Mind
Translating English sentences into First-Order Logic requires identifying the correct quantifiers and the correct logical connectives that pair with them.
The Golden Rules of Translation:
1.  **Universal Quantifier ($\forall$) almost ALWAYS pairs with Implication ($\rightarrow$).** "All dogs are mammals" $\equiv \forall x (Dog(x) \rightarrow Mammal(x))$.
2.  **Existential Quantifier ($\exists$) almost ALWAYS pairs with Conjunction ($\land$).** "Some dogs are brown" $\equiv \exists x (Dog(x) \land Brown(x))$.
</ChapterCard>

<GateTrap>
**Trap: The Fatal Mismatch**
The most common mistake in GATE is mixing the golden pairings:
*   $\forall x (Dog(x) \land Mammal(x))$ means "Everything in the entire universe is a dog AND a mammal." (Obviously false).
*   $\exists x (Dog(x) \rightarrow Brown(x))$ is a vacuous trap. Because $P \rightarrow Q$ is true when $P$ is false, this statement is true as long as there is *at least one thing in the universe that is NOT a dog*. It does not guarantee a brown dog exists at all!
</GateTrap>

<ArchitectShortcut>
**Shortcut: The "Not All" and "None" Templates**
Memorize these exact templates to bypass slow mental translation:
*   "Not all A are B" $\equiv \neg \forall x(A(x) \rightarrow B(x)) \equiv \exists x(A(x) \land \neg B(x))$ ("There is some A that is not B").
*   "No A are B" $\equiv \forall x(A(x) \rightarrow \neg B(x)) \equiv \neg \exists x(A(x) \land B(x))$ ("There does not exist an A that is also B").
</ArchitectShortcut>

### The Proving Ground

**Question 1 (NAT):**
Consider the statement: "Every boy loves some girl." Let $B(x)$ be "$x$ is a boy", $G(y)$ be "$y$ is a girl", and $L(x,y)$ be "$x$ loves $y$". Which of the following is correct?
(A) $\forall x \exists y (B(x) \land G(y) \land L(x,y))$
(B) $\forall x (B(x) \rightarrow \exists y (G(y) \land L(x,y)))$
*(Answer 1 for A, 2 for B).*

**Question 2 (NAT):**
Translate: "There is no student who knows every professor." Let $S(x)$ be student, $P(y)$ be professor, $K(x,y)$ be knows. Is $\neg \exists x (S(x) \land \forall y (P(y) \rightarrow K(x,y)))$ logically equivalent to the English sentence? (Answer 1 for Yes, 0 for No).
