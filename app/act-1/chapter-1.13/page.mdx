# Chapter 1.13: Logical Equivalences

<ChapterCard>
### The Briefing: The Algebra of Propositions
Two propositions $X$ and $Y$ are logically equivalent (written as $X \equiv Y$) if they have identical truth values for all possible assignments of their variables. This means the biconditional $X \leftrightarrow Y$ is a tautology.
Instead of relying on truth tables, which grow exponentially ($2^n$ rows for $n$ variables), we use standard equivalence laws to simplify expressions analytically:
* **Double Negation:** $\neg(\neg P) \equiv P$
* **De Morgan's:** $\neg(P \land Q) \equiv \neg P \lor \neg Q$
* **Distributive:** $P \lor (Q \land R) \equiv (P \lor Q) \land (P \lor R)$
* **Absorption:** $P \lor (P \land Q) \equiv P$
</ChapterCard>

<GateTrap>
**Trap: Overlooking the OR-Distribution**
Students are comfortable distributing AND over OR because it mimics standard multiplication over addition: $A(B+C) = AB + AC$. 
However, Propositional Logic also allows distributing OR over AND: $A + (BC) = (A+B)(A+C)$. Forgetting this law often traps students in dead-end simplifications during exams.
</GateTrap>

<ArchitectShortcut>
**Shortcut: The Implication Chain**
When faced with chained implications like $P \rightarrow (Q \rightarrow R)$, never try to evaluate them in your head. Immediately convert them to ORs.
$P \rightarrow (\neg Q \lor R)$
$\neg P \lor (\neg Q \lor R)$
Since all operators are now ORs, the parentheses can be dropped (Associative Law). The expression is simply $\neg P \lor \neg Q \lor R$.
</ArchitectShortcut>
